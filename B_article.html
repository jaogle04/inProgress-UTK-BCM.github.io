Fall of 2023

Who We Are at the B

It's just another newsletter.... or is it?

Editor in Chief: Whitney Stricklin

Of all the places on campus, UTK's Baptist Collegiate Ministry (BCM) seems to always be full of life and activities. Students come and go throughout the day to study, eat, chat, and sometimes… take a nap. Our little campus house on Melrose Avenue is packed with every kind of person. Any room you walk into, you'll find college students from all walks of life fellowshipping with one another. From Soul Food to spiritual interest surveys, our group is ready to serve this campus any way we can to bring people closer to Jesus. With attendance each Thursday night- our worship session called “TNT” -reaching about 100+ students each week, the opportunities to meet new people and build community continue to rise. What better way to hear about what happens at the BCM than from our very own students? The B was created to bring a more personal touch to our latest news.

Releasing once a semester, you will be able to read columns from undergraduates journaling their experiences in life, missions, and a whole lotta Jesus. We want to connect more with our supporters and fellow students; that's why you're receiving this newsletter today .With an inside look at everything this ministry does, we hope you will find this student journal uplifting; because we can't do it without you! Prayer is of utmost importance to us here at the BCM. Prayer gives life to ministry and fuels every step of the Christian walk. In reading these upcoming testimonials from our students, we wish to provide you with specific things to pray for.

Freshman Spotlight

Contributor: Braden Bright

My experience at the BCM so far has been nothing but awesome. The BCM offers an environment for people to get closer to Christ, while also making life-long friendships. They also offer life groups, which are by far one of the most critical groups a college student could get involved in. I have met some of my closest friends in my life group and my faith has 100% been strengthened because of [it]. Additionally, the BCM holds a worship/service every Thursday night at 7:00 called TNT (Thursday Nights Together). For me, this is a time to clear my thoughts and feel a peace that I cannot feel any other way except through worship. I am looking forward to what the future holds for me being a part of the BCM, and all of the memories I will inevitably make.

Trin's Spin on Fall Retreat

Contributor: Trinity Sankey

Fall Retreat 2023 with the BCM was an eye-opening, perspective-changing experience that I had the privilege of attending. For those who do not know, Fall Retreat is an overnight weekend retreat the BCM takes during the fall semester. It is a chance to take time with God, learn about different topics, and meet others who attend the BCM by doing group bonding. Themed retreats are my favorite and the BCM did not disappoint. The theme for the weekend was all things toxic. Those who were there got to hear about toxic culture, toxic femininity, toxic masculinity, toxic religion, and much more. The weekend allowed for opportunities to learn about the toxicity that consumes the world and have a chance to dissect it with peers. Learning about the different topics within toxicity was a highlight of the weekend, but other parts have stuck with me since the end of the retreat. During the retreat, current students who attend the BCM lead panels and devotions that challenge us to ask questions and dive deeper into other topics such as greed, pride, and social media in our own lives. One topic in particular, pride, was an interesting topic that opened the door to the different stages associated with pride. It was something that I had never considered. Most people try not to be prideful, but the different levels of pride such as self-exaltation, self-promotion, self-justification, self-degradation, self-demotion, and self-condemnation. These various stages of pride challenge the definition people recognize. For example, putting yourself down in an unhealthy way is an example of one of the stages of pride: self-condemnation. Learning about the different levels of pride was an important part of the Fall Retreat for me because no one is perfect; we all fall subject to one thing or another as we go through life.

Another part I loved was the toxic femininity talk with all the BCM girls led by Samm Hawes. One takeaway I learned was humility does not mean making yourself small, but instead giving God all the glory in your life. This idea of humility is a perspective change that I took into consideration and had to reflect on my life. The best part of the weekend, despite everything I already mentioned, was having my quiet time in a peaceful, beautiful environment where it was just me and God. Everyone's quiet times can be different, and for me, I like to take a moment and just silently exist in the world God created and listen to life on Earth. This personal moment with God during Fall Retreat provided me with certainty that I am where God needs me to be in life and I am one of His many creations. My moment with God is not to discourage others who might not have similar experiences or moments alone with God but is a reminder that God does love and provide for all of His children in different ways. Every Fall Retreat experience varies from person to person. But for me, I changed my perspectives of how I view certain things and I let God take those burdens. So, if you are considering taking a leap of faith into the unknown, or just deciding if attending a retreat is worth it, I suggest you pray about it and trust God, because a large leap for you can be a small step towards God!

To Lead is to Serve


In November, the BCM was able to participate in our 2nd annual ServeKnox Week! Students had the opportunity to serve at many different locations. We were able to help prepare toys and clothes to send to children in rural Appalachia through Mission of Hope, write notes of encouragement for friends and family, host a time of worship and fellowship at a retirement community, continue to support Montgomery Village and Western Heights children's bible studies, fellowship and pray for staff at various international restaurants around Knoxville, and help run games for Pond Gap Elementary's fall festival. We saw a lot of fruitful conversations take place as well as many physical needs met. It has been so encouraging to see the way that BCM students are passionate about serving one another and their community.

So many of the students involved at the BCM are serving in some capacity whether it is leading a life group, teaching ESL, running the tech and lights for TNT, or getting up early to make hot chocolate for Coffee Cart. Our students do a wonderful job following Jesus's example in serving others. When thinking about what it means to serve, I feel that it is always important to reorient ourselves to the gospel and remind ourselves of what the Lord has done for us. Jesus loved us so much that He was willing to face the wrath of God to redeem and reunite our sinful and unrighteous souls with Himself. I believe that as we gaze upon the gospel and what the Lord has done for us, we will have a deep desire to follow and serve the Lord out of love for our great savior. Serving others does not earn us salvation or God's approval, we have that through Jesus Christ alone. Serving others is a natural overflow of the love that we have been shown and a desire to obey the One who saved our lives. In light of this truth, we can respond in obedience to Jesus's call to care for those around us in passages like Mathew 25:35-45. Furthermore, we can persevere in doing good like Galatians 6:9 calls us to do. We love because He first loved us. We serve because we were made new. The God who redeemed us and called us to himself is worthy of all of our praise, our service, and our lives.

Behind the Booth

Serving Outside of the Spotlight

Contributor: Turner Perry

At the beginning of this year, I jokingly told our worship team that our scripture verse for the year should be “He looked upon their distress when He heard their cry” (Psalm 106:44). This scripture had always seemed to ring true as we finished out our previous school year and, while comedic, felt like an accurate way of describing life in the booth when gearing up for a new semester. Working in the booth is an interesting collection of paradoxes. You are involved in every aspect of the service, yet doing your job well means you should go completely unnoticed. Humility is quite literally the job description because the goal of everything done in the booth is to lead the body to connect with Christ. We use amps and microphones so that everyone in the body can clearly hear the words both spoken and sung, we use projectors for our lyrics so that the body knows the lyrics to sing, and we use lights to focus our attention so that the room is not a distraction. Each role in the booth exists for a specific purpose, not to glorify ourselves, our band, or guest speakers, but to center the body's focus on God's glory and presence.

While we use our jobs in the booth to establish order and familiarity within our worship services, you can never fully mitigate chaos. From mics going out mid-sermon to ProPresenter shutting down three minutes before the song service, there is no way to anticipate what wrinkle may occur from week to week. While you desperately try to account for these factors, you must be flexible and prepared to change course at a moment's notice. It is in these moments that you must remember that God's glory is the number one goal. Personally, if my perspective shifts from our goal to my stresses, I immediately start to limit God's glory to my perception of excellence rather than the glory He always displays and deserves. In Haggai, Israel was rebuilding the temple but struggled with the fact that it would not be as grand as Solomon's temple. However, God made it clear to the people that their obedience and faithfulness was the most beautiful temple they could build to him within their hearts. Later, God proclaimed that he would shake the other nations and provide the very wealth and jewels that the Israelites felt they could not account for. Like the rest of our Christian walk, excellence should not paralyze us, but embolden us in the pursuit of the one who is truly excellent and is imparting his own nature to us. While the booth is a new adventure each week, it also brings the grandest blessings. You get to see every hand that is raised or head that is bowed during the song service, or the way classmates minister to and pray over one another when they have had a tough week. When the music gets quiet, you can hear every voice in the room ringing out in worship and recognition of their Savior. Songs that were tricky during rehearsal come together seamlessly through the Lord's faithfulness and reach the body so personally. Ultimately, the story of the booth is striving to lead the body to meet the Lord, trusting in His faithfulness to fill in the gaps and faults of our humanity.
